How Partial-Flow MicroGPT Works

This app trains a neural network to learn Manning's equation for partially-full circular pipes. The key twist: unlike full-pipe flow, the relationship between depth and flow is non-monotonic — flow increases with depth until d/D ≈ 0.938, then decreases as the pipe approaches full. The network must discover this hidden peak from data alone.

This is the direct sequel to Manning's MicroGPT, adding one input dimension (d/D) that transforms a smooth power law into a function with a hidden maximum. Most engineers know about full-pipe flow but many don't intuitively realize that a pipe at 93.8% depth carries MORE flow than when completely full.

The Geometry — Why the Peak Exists

For a circular pipe of diameter D at depth d, the central angle is θ = 2·acos(1 − 2d/D). All geometric properties follow from θ:

Flow Area
A = (D²/8)(θ − sin θ)
The circular segment area. Grows rapidly at first, then slows as the pipe approaches full.
Wetted Perimeter
Pw = Dθ/2
The arc length in contact with water. Grows linearly with θ — this is the key. Near the crown, P grows faster than A.

Hydraulic radius Rh = A/Pw. Since Q ∝ A × Rh2/3, the peak occurs where the combined A × Rh2/3 term is maximized. Near the crown, the wetted perimeter wraps around the top of the pipe faster than the remaining area fills in, causing Rh to drop — and Q with it.

The exact peak is at d/D = 0.938 where Q/Qfull ≈ 1.076. This means a pipe at 93.8% depth carries 7.6% more flow than when completely full!

Why This Matters for SWMM5

SWMM5 computes partial-depth flow at every conduit at every time step. The normal depth computation uses bisection to find the depth where Manning's Q matches the required flow. Understanding the Q vs d/D curve — especially the peak — is essential for interpreting surcharge conditions. When SWMM5 reports d/D > 1.0, the pipe is pressurized; when d/D ≈ 0.94 and Q is at capacity, you're actually at the most efficient depth.

The Manning's MicroGPT → Partial-Flow Connection

Manning's MicroGPT learned Q = f(n, D, S) for full-pipe flow — 3 inputs, all smooth power laws. Partial-Flow MicroGPT adds one dimension (d/D) that transforms the smooth function into one with a hidden maximum. This is a fundamentally different learning challenge: the network must discover that more depth doesn't always mean more flow. ReLU activations are well-suited to learn this peak because they naturally create piecewise-linear approximations.

Why Engineers Miss the Peak

The peak at d/D ≈ 0.938 is counterintuitive because we think "more water = more flow." But hydraulic radius Rh = A/Pw, and near the pipe crown the perimeter grows faster than the area. It's like adding a thin sliver of water that touches a lot of pipe wall but doesn't add much cross-section. The friction from the extra wall contact outweighs the extra area. This is also why V/Vfull peaks even earlier (d/D ≈ 0.813) — velocity is Q/A, and the area near the crown contributes little velocity.
